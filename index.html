Why Does Seniority Always Win?

A mathematical model of workplace rank dynamics. Even when younger people are more capable, organizations reliably re-sort back into age order — and this simulation shows exactly why.

Open Simulation Read the Theory
How it works

Each dot is an employee. Their horizontal position is fixed by tenure (how long they've been around). Their vertical position is rank — the only thing that moves. Color shows competence (blue = lower, red = higher). When the dots line up along the diagonal, rank perfectly matches tenure: the senior person is on top.

Three forces tug on each dot's rank every frame:

α · merit
Meritocratic drift
Pulls your rank toward your competence level. Small in most real organizations.
β · seniority
Seniority spring
Pulls rank back to what your tenure predicts — like a spring. Captures deferred pay contracts and fairness norms.
γ · holdup
Insider holdup
Pushes juniors back down when they outrank a senior. Captures the "hostage" power of institutional knowledge.
drᵢ = [ α(cᵢ − c̄)  −  β(rᵢ − sᵢ)  −  γHᵢ ] dt  +  σ dWᵢ
sᵢ = rank predicted by tenure alone  ·  Hᵢ = holdup pressure from displaced seniors  ·  σ dWᵢ = noise (politics, luck)

The key ratio is α/β. Below ~0.5 → seniority wins. Above ~2 → meritocracy. Most real organizations sit around 0.1–0.3. Full theory →
